to stay sharp

Not a beginner's guide. This is the stack I use working at the intersection of law, policy, and AI governance — primary sources, serious institutions, and the people worth following when you want analysis, not hype.

01

Technical foundations

01
But what is a neural network? — 3Blue1BrownFreeVideo

The clearest visual explanation of how neural networks function. Before engaging with AI regulation, you need to understand what you're regulating. This is where to start — not dumbed down, not gatekept.

↗ 02
What is ChatGPT doing? — Stephen WolframFreeLong read

Mechanically explains how large language models produce text. If you use "hallucination" as a policy term without understanding why it happens mechanically, read this first. Long, rigorous, worth every minute.

↗ 03
AI For Everyone — Andrew NgAudit freeCourse

Designed for decision-makers, not engineers. Ng explains what AI can and cannot do in terms a policy practitioner can use. The section on what makes a viable AI project translates directly to procurement and governance advisory work.

↗ 04
Elements of AI — University of HelsinkiFreeCourse

The most accessible foundation available. If you need to bring colleagues or clients up to speed before a governance conversation, this is the link to send them. Built by an institution with no commercial interest in your conclusion.

02

Primary sources

01
EU AI Act — Regulation 2024/1689Primary source

Read Annex III and Article 6 before anything else — that is where the classification logic lives. The recitals carry interpretive weight that most commentary ignores. If you advise on EU-market AI compliance, there is no substitute for the actual text.

↗ 02
EU AI Office — Implementation & GuidancePrimary source

The enforcement body for the AI Act on GPAI models. Guidance documents here are more operationally useful than academic commentary speculating on what the Act "probably means." Track what it actually publishes.

↗ 03
NIST AI Risk Management Framework 1.0Primary source

The most operationally useful risk document available. The GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE structure gives practitioners concrete vocabulary for advising organisations on internal AI governance — regardless of jurisdiction.

↗ 04
OECD AI Principles & Policy ObservatoryFramework

The OECD AI Principles underpin most national AI strategies. The Observatory tracks implementation across 70+ countries — essential for comparative governance work. The live policy database is a genuine research time-saver.

↗
03

Governance research

01
Stanford AI Index Report — AnnualFreeReport

The most data-dense annual snapshot of where AI actually stands. Grounds governance arguments in empirical reality rather than lobbying narratives. The policy chapter is the one I read first each year.

↗ 02
Center for Security & Emerging Technology — GeorgetownFreeThink tank

Rigorous, non-partisan analysis on AI talent, compute governance, and geopolitics. If your work touches export controls, AI in defence, or US-China technology competition, CSET is the reference point — not the newspaper.

↗ 03
AI Now InstituteFreeResearch

Critical, social-science-grounded research on algorithmic accountability, civil rights, and labour. Necessary counterweight to purely technical or purely regulatory framings. Their annual reports on AI in public sector deployment are required reading.

↗ 04
Global Partnership on AI (GPAI)FreeResearch hub

Intergovernmental body producing working group reports on responsible AI and data governance. Less cited than it deserves. The working group outputs are often more practically grounded than academic papers covering the same questions.

↗
04

Legal practice

01
Law Society — Technology & the LawFreeGuidance

UK-anchored but globally relevant ethics guidance on AI in legal practice. One of the clearest professional responsibility frameworks available. Useful for understanding what regulators are watching before they start enforcing it.

↗ 02
Harvard Law — Cyberlaw ClinicFreeAcademic

Working papers and commentary on AI liability, copyright, due process, and platform governance. Dense but reliable for academic citations when you need weight behind an AI legal argument.

↗ 03
ABA Legal Technology Survey — AnnualFree (summary)Report

Empirical data on how lawyers are actually adopting AI — adoption rates, use cases, ethics concerns. The gap between industry discourse and ground reality is instructive for anyone advising bar associations or legal aid organisations.

↗ 04
AI Governance — University of Virginia / CourseraAudit freeCourse

One of the few courses where governance is the actual subject, not an appendix to an engineering syllabus. Covers risk frameworks, ethics, and regulatory approaches in a sequence that works for practitioners.

↗
05

Tools worth knowing

01
LexisNexis AILegal AISubscription

The dominant legal research platform's AI layer. If you advise firms on AI tool adoption or risk, this is the system most practitioners encounter. Understanding its architecture matters for understanding where liability exposure actually sits.

↗ 02
Casetext / CoCounsel — Thomson ReutersLegal AISubscription

AI-assisted research and contract analysis. One of the more technically mature legal AI products. The Thomson Reuters acquisition signals where incumbents are positioning. Knowing what it can't do is as important as knowing what it can.

↗ 03
ElicitResearch toolFree tier

AI-assisted academic literature search. Surfaces papers and extracts findings. Better for policy and governance research than for case law. Useful when you need to know what the published scholarship says on a question before citing it.

↗ 04
Perplexity AIResearch toolFree tier

AI search with inline citations. More epistemically honest than most about uncertainty. Useful for rapid background research when you need sourced starting points, not unsourced assertions. Verify everything before you cite.

06

People worth following

Kate Crawford

Author of Atlas of AI. The most serious political economy analysis of AI's material costs and power implications.

Arvind Narayanan

Princeton. Consistently honest about what AI can and cannot do. Read him before writing any capability claim into a policy document.

Timnit Gebru

AI ethics and algorithmic harm. Critical, rigorous, and correct more often than the institutions that pushed her out.

Benedict Evans

Tech strategy and structural change. Less hype, more market reality. Good antidote to governance proposals built on sci-fi assumptions.

Yoshua Bengio

Turing Award laureate turned AI safety advocate. His submissions to government inquiries are models of how technical expertise translates into policy language.

Helen Toner

Georgetown CSET. Former OpenAI board member. Sharp on where AI governance accountability actually sits versus where it appears to sit.
