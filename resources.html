Resource library · AI & Legal Tech

The reading list I wish
I had when I started

Most people approaching AI from a legal background don't know where to begin. Most people approaching AI from a technical background don't know what legal actually means. This list is for both — curated from what I've actually used, read, and found worth the time. My take is next to each one. Nothing here is paid placement.

6 Categories
30+ Resources
Updated Regularly
01

Start here

01
But what is a neural network? — 3Blue1Brown Free Video

The best visual explanation of how neural networks actually work. Not dumbed down, not intimidating. If you watch one thing before reading any policy document, make it this. The rest of the series is equally good.

↗ 02
What is generative AI? — MIT Technology Review Free Article

Clear, no-jargon explainer from a source that doesn't have an agenda to sell you anything. Covers generative AI specifically — which is the piece most governance discussions are actually about.

↗ 03
What is ChatGPT doing? — Stephen Wolfram Free Long read

Long. Worth every minute. Goes deeper than any other accessible explanation on how LLMs produce text. If you want to understand what "hallucination" actually means mechanically — not just as a buzzword — read this.

↗ 04
Practical Deep Learning for Coders — fast.ai Free Course

You don't have to do the whole thing. Even the first two lectures give you enough to stop nodding blankly when engineers talk. For lawyers who want genuine technical literacy rather than surface familiarity.

↗
02

AI Governance & Policy

01
EU AI Act — Full Text Primary source

The actual legislation. Most commentary misses nuance that only shows up if you read the recitals. Annex III (high-risk systems) and Article 6 (classification rules) are the two things everyone citing this statute should actually understand.

↗ 02
Stanford AI Index Report — Annual Free Report

The most data-dense annual snapshot of where AI actually stands — research output, investment, policy activity, public perception. Useful for grounding any governance argument in what's actually happening rather than what feels true.

↗ 03
US Executive Order on AI (Oct 2023) Primary source

The reference point for US federal AI policy at the executive level. The contrast with the EU's risk-classification approach is instructive — and that contrast is where most of the comparative governance literature lives.

↗ 04
Global Partnership on AI (GPAI) Free Research hub

Intergovernmental body producing working group reports on AI governance, responsible AI, and data governance. Less cited than it should be. The working group outputs are often more practically useful than academic papers on the same questions.

↗ 05
AI Now Institute Free Research

Critical, social-science-grounded AI research. Particularly good on algorithmic accountability and the labour and civil rights dimensions of AI deployment. Useful counterweight to purely technical or purely regulatory framings.

↗
03

For Legal Professionals

01
ABA Legal Technology Survey Report Free (summary) Report

Annual survey of how lawyers are actually using technology — adoption rates, concerns, use cases. Useful for understanding where the profession is, not just where the discourse thinks it is.

↗ 02
Law Society — Technology & the Law Free Guidance

UK-focused but globally relevant guidance on AI use in legal practice, including ethics considerations and professional responsibility questions. Good for understanding what regulators are actually paying attention to.

↗ 03
Harvard Law — Cyberlaw Clinic & LPE Project Free Academic

Harvard's law and technology output — working papers, clinic reports, and commentary. Dense but reliable. Worth bookmarking for when you need a credible citation on AI liability, intellectual property, or due process questions.

↗ 04
Rice University — Future Lawyer Initiative Free Resource hub

Practical skills and resources for law students navigating a legal market that is changing faster than law schools are updating their curricula. Specifically useful if you are in law school and trying to figure out what to actually learn.

↗
04

Courses worth finishing

01
Google Data Analytics Professional Certificate Paid / Financial aid available

I did this one. It teaches you to think analytically about data, not just run tools. For lawyers, the SQL and data cleaning modules are the most immediately useful. The certificate itself is recognized; the skills matter more.

↗ 02
AI For Everyone — Andrew Ng / DeepLearning.AI Paid / Audit free

Non-technical. That's the point. Ng is one of the clearest communicators in AI and this course is designed specifically for people who need to understand AI to make decisions about it, not build it. Six hours well spent.

↗ 03
CS50 AI — Harvard / edX Free to audit

More technical than the others on this list. But if you want to understand what search algorithms, machine learning, and neural networks actually do — not just what they're called — this builds it from first principles. Challenging. Worth it.

↗ 04
AI Governance — University of Virginia / Coursera Paid / Audit free

One of the few courses that treats governance as the central subject rather than an afterthought to an AI engineering course. Covers risk frameworks, ethics, and regulatory approaches. Directly relevant to what I work on.

↗ 05
Elements of AI — University of Helsinki Free

Genuinely the most accessible AI introduction I have found. Built for people with no technical background. If you have a colleague, supervisor, or client who needs to understand AI before they can have a useful conversation about it — send them here first.

↗
05

Tools legal professionals
are encountering

01
LexisNexis AI Legal AI Subscription

The incumbent legal research database adding AI features. If you are going into practice at a firm of any size, this is the system you will encounter. Worth understanding how it works before you trust its outputs in a submission.

↗ 02
Casetext / CoCounsel Legal AI Subscription

AI-assisted legal research and document review. Acquired by Thomson Reuters. One of the more mature legal AI tools. The document analysis features are where it earns its money. Worth knowing what it can and cannot do — especially the cannot.

↗ 03
Perplexity AI Research tool Free tier

AI-powered search with citations. More honest about uncertainty than most. Useful for quick background research when you need sources, not just outputs. Still verify everything — but the cited format makes verification faster.

↗ 04
Elicit Research tool Free tier

AI research assistant that searches academic papers and surfaces relevant findings. Better for policy research than for case law — it lives in the academic literature layer. Useful for comparative governance work when you need to know what the scholars are saying.

↗
06

People worth reading

3Blue1Brown (Grant Sanderson)

Math & neural network visualizations. Best technical communicator working today.

Timnit Gebru

AI ethics and algorithmic harm. Critical, rigorous, and right more often than not.

Benedict Evans

Tech strategy and what AI actually changes about industry. Less hype than most.

Ian Kerr

Law and AI. One of the few legal academics who actually understands the technology.

Yann LeCun

Chief AI Scientist at Meta. Contrarian on AI doom, useful corrective to the hype cycle.

Arvind Narayanan

Princeton CS professor. Honest about what AI can and cannot do. Worth reading before policy work.

A note on curation: This list reflects what I have personally found useful working at the intersection of law and AI. It is not exhaustive and it is not neutral — I have opinions about what is worth your time and I have put them here. Nothing is sponsored. I update this when something better comes along or when a link dies. If you think something belongs here that isn't here, get in touch.
