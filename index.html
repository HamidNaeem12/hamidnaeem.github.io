About me

The interesting question isn't whether AI will change the law — it's already doing that. The question is who does the analytical work to keep up with it. That's what I focus on.

My background combines legal training (LL.B) with hands-on data work. That combination isn't common, and it turns out to be precisely what AI governance analysis needs: someone who can read a model card and a statute in the same sitting, and produce output that's useful to both audiences.

Over the past year I've worked across legal environments in Pakistan, India, the UAE, and the US — on everything from civil litigation and family law to comparative regulatory research on the EU AI Act and US executive orders. I've drafted documents for active cases, co-authored policy papers, built a legal content platform with a cross-border readership, and published a book.

My practice is deliberately cross-disciplinary. I use Python and data tools to find patterns in legal datasets that pure doctrinal analysis misses. And I bring legal precision to data work that would otherwise ignore the regulatory environment it operates in.
