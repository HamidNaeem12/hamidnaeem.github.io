About

The work I do

AI regulation is moving fast. Most organizations are somewhere between "we should probably look into this" and "we have no idea what we're doing." I write the analysis that helps close that gap.

My background is LL.B plus hands-on data work — Python, dashboards, legal datasets. That combination is rarer than it should be. I can read a model card and a statute in the same sitting and tell you what the mismatch actually means for compliance obligations in your jurisdiction.

Over the past year I have worked across Pakistan, India, the UAE, and the US — civil litigation, family law, comparative regulatory research, policy drafting. I built a legal platform read by 570+ professionals across three continents and published a book. Cross-jurisdictional work was a byproduct of opportunity at first; it became deliberate once I realized how rare it was.

The Global South regulatory context is underrepresented in the conversations that matter. Most AI governance writing comes from Brussels, London, or Washington. I work from the opposite direction — asking what the EU AI Act actually means when the regulator is the FIA, not the ICO. That gap is where I operate.

Unpacking Justice: A Deep Dive into PLD 1966 Supreme Court 708 and the Pursuit of Truth in Criminal Trials

What a sixty-year-old Supreme Court decision still reveals about how evidence is weighed in Pakistan's criminal courts.

By Hamid Naeem · Veritas and Vows · July 14, 2025

At Veritas and Vows, we're committed to shedding light on the intricacies of the law, ensuring clarity and empowering understanding. Today, we delve into a landmark decision from the Supreme Court of Pakistan, PLD 1966 Supreme Court 708, a judgment that grappled with fundamental procedural challenges in criminal trials and offers enduring lessons on the pursuit of truth and fair legal processes.

This case, stemming from Criminal Appeals Nos. 8 and 9 of 1965, brought before the Supreme Court by Nur Elahi, addressed a critical question: how should courts handle murder cases where two entirely separate versions of the incident, implicating different sets of accused, are presented through a private complaint and a police challan (report)?

The Core Dilemma: Conflicting Narratives and Procedural Sanction

The High Court of West Pakistan had initially laid down a "special procedure" for these trials, recommending that common witnesses be examined only once and their statements be read as evidence in the other case. This approach, while seemingly efficient, became the central point of contention for the Supreme Court. The core question on appeal was whether this "special procedure" had the "sanction of law".

Key Holdings and Their Significance

1. Rejection of Inter-Case Evidence Reading

The Supreme Court unequivocally held that directing witnesses to be examined only once and their statements read out as evidence in the other case is not supportable in law. Justice S. A. Rahman, speaking for the majority, emphasized that reading evidence recorded in another case as evidence in the case at hand is strictly debarred. Violation of this procedural boundary completely invalidates the legal finality of the trial.

"Every criminal proceeding is to be decided on the material on record of that proceeding and neither record of another case nor any finding recorded there-under should affect the decision and if the Court takes into consideration evidence recorded in other case, a finding recorded there in judgment is vitiated."

— PLD 1966 Supreme Court 708, p. 710

2. Separate Trials, Sequential Approach

The Court affirmed the High Court's view that a joint trial of two sets of accused, where they are not involved in the same transaction, contravenes Section 239 of the Code of Criminal Procedure. To navigate the complexities of two distinct versions, the majority proposed a strict sequential procedure:

The Sequential Protocol:
  1. The complaint case should be taken up first for trial.
  2. During the complaint case, the trial judge may call witnesses mentioned in the police challan as Court witnesses under Section 540-A of the Criminal Procedure Code, allowing cross-examination by both parties.
  3. If the first trial results in a conviction, the Public Prosecutor would then consider withdrawing the police challan case under Section 494 of the Criminal Procedure Code.
  4. If the first case results in an acquittal, the second trial (police challan) would proceed to its conclusion.

3. Dissenting View on "Consolidation" and Public Prosecutor's Role

Justice B. Z. Kaikaus, in his dissenting opinion, highlighted the difficulties with the majority's proposed procedure. He argued that allowing a decision in one case to influence the handling of another, even implicitly, risks disregarding fundamental principles of justice.

Why This Matters Today

  • Safeguarding Fair Trial: The judgment powerfully reaffirms that each case must stand on its own evidence, protecting against prejudice that could arise from findings in separate proceedings.
  • Navigating Parallel Proceedings: In a legal landscape where multiple complaints or versions of an incident can arise, this judgment provides a framework for managing such complexities in criminal matters.
  • The Role of the Public Prosecutor: The discussion underscores the delicate balance between the prosecutor's duty to the State and their responsibility to ensure justice is served.
